# Model Compression

Model compression techniques reduce a trained model's size and/or inference latency, usually to meet a deployment constraint (mobile device memory, edge hardware, real-time latency budgets, or serving cost at scale) that the original full-size model can't satisfy.

Quantization reduces the numerical precision used to represent a model's weights and activations, typically from 32-bit floating point down to 16-bit float, 8-bit integer, or even lower (4-bit is common for large language models today). Post-training quantization is applied after training completes and is fast to apply but can lose more accuracy; quantization-aware training simulates the precision reduction during training itself, letting the model adapt its weights to be more robust to the eventual precision loss, usually recovering most of the accuracy gap.

Pruning removes weights, neurons, or entire structural components (attention heads, channels) that contribute the least to the model's output, based on some importance criterion (typically magnitude — small weights are assumed less important). Unstructured pruning removes individual weights and can achieve high sparsity but often needs specialized hardware/kernels to realize actual speedup, since dense hardware doesn't skip zeros for free. Structured pruning removes entire channels or heads, which is less flexible but yields real speedups on standard hardware because the resulting model is just smaller, not sparse.

Knowledge distillation trains a smaller "student" model to mimic a larger, more accurate "teacher" model's outputs (often its soft probability distribution over classes, not just the hard label), rather than training the student from scratch on the original labels alone. The soft targets carry more information than one-hot labels — they encode the teacher's uncertainty and relative confidence across classes — which typically lets the student reach higher accuracy than training on hard labels alone would achieve at the same model size.

Low-rank factorization decomposes large weight matrices into products of smaller matrices with a lower intrinsic rank, reducing parameter count and compute. This is the same mathematical idea behind LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation), widely used to fine-tune large language models efficiently by learning small low-rank update matrices instead of updating all original weights.

A common interview question: "You need to deploy a model on a mobile device with a strict 50MB size limit and a 100ms latency budget. Walk through your approach." Answer: start by measuring which constraint actually binds first — size or latency — since they call for different techniques. Quantization is usually the first, cheapest lever (often a 4x size reduction with modest accuracy loss going int8), followed by structured pruning if more size reduction is needed, and distillation into a purpose-built smaller architecture if the target device's constraints are severe enough that no amount of compressing the original architecture gets there.
